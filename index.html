Clearing the Way to Conflict: Deforestation, Downwind Rainfall and Violence

with T. Vendryes and J. Wolfersberger

Rainfall shocks shape development and conflict outcomes, yet their drivers are often assumed to be exogenous. In this paper, we show that deforestation in West Africa reduces precipitation in downwind areas and causes conflict. Combining satellite forest loss, wind trajectories, precipitation, and conflict events since the 2000s, we construct location-specific exposure to upstream deforestation using prevailing winds and exclude nearby pixels to isolate long-distance spillovers. We find that an increase in upstream deforestation indeed lowers precipitation, with effects extending to 200 km. Turning to development outcomes, we show that exposure to deforestation increases conflict intensity in downwind areas, driven primarily by civil unrest events, with effects persisting for up to six years.

Previous Research

Master 2 Thesis: Electricity Access and Agricultural Productivity in Madagascar under the supervision of Mathilde Maurel

This paper estimates the heterogeneous and dynamic effects of first-time electricity access on cropland productivity in Madagascar using satellite data in a staggered difference-in-differences setup. I show that while initial impacts may be negative, long-term benefits arise, particularly in villages located above productive aquifers.

Master 1 Thesis: Water Pollution and Infant Health in India under the supervision of Thomas Vendryes

This paper examines how water pollution affects child health in India, showing that higher levels of specific pollutants are associated with lower birth weight and poorer growth outcomes, based on a newly constructed water pollution level dataset matched with DHS data.
